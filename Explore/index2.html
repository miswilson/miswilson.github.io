    Overview

    My portfolio collates my completed collection of charts and data visualisations.
    Aims: Through this portfolio I aim to demonstrate the successful completion of my visualisation. The outcomes of my charts are representative of the 10 coding challenges - I strive to showcase my ability to analyse and interpret data effectively.

    Ever Changing Trends: Does the fashion industry follow the same cyclical cycle of economic booms and busts?
    Exploring the relationship between the state of the economy and fashion trends, through changing silhouettes, extravagant designs, and developing textiles, across countries.

    I have always kept close analysis on clothing silhouettes and the cycles that follow trends revolving around fashion. Undoubtedly this aligns with culture, particularly by country - as individuals dress accordingly to their countrys' climate and conditions. What is often overlooked, is the correlation between the economy and individuals' consciousness to dress based on the stability and unpredictability of economic growth. My ambitions in analytics and observing data has led me to explore the tangents between booms and busts in the economy and trending expressionism through fashion at the same time. The correlation between culture and fashion is proven to be closely correlated: for instance, in the 1940s the flourishment of the design of the suit for females, and the movement towards a trouser, over a skirt, for practicality. This corresponds to an increase in female labour force participation. To complete both my portfolio and project I have used several data sources.

    Exploring Scatter Charts

    The scatter plot below visualises the relationship between GDP per capita (on a logarithmic scale) and life expectancy, across different regions of the world. The x-axis presents GDP per capita (PPP, international $), whilst the y-axis illustrates life expectancy in years. Each point represents a country - by region coded by colour - and the size of the points reflects population size. The data follows the general trend that wealthier countries have better health outcomes, as indicated by higher life expectancies. By eye, countries in Africa are concentrated in the ranges with lower GDP and life expectancy, whilst Europe and parts of Oceania are more concentrated in the upper, right-hand region of the chart. This demonstrates the correlation between higher GDP and life expectancy.

    Exploring Line Charts

    This line chart effectively illustrates the widening global economic disparities, using PPP rates of Real GDP per Capita ($) from 1990 to 2022. Across the six countries it can be seen that the rapid rise of China stands out.

    The data reads that the US and UK show consistently high GDP per capita, with the UK experiencing sharper growth after 2015, reaching around $70,000 in 2022. China follows a more significant growth trajectory, especially after 2005, rising sharply to over $20,000 by 2022. India, Nigeria, and Congo remain much lower in comparison, though all three exhibit gradual upward trends. The gap between more developed economies (US, UK) and developing countries (Congo, Nigeria, India) remains substantial throughout the period.

    Comparing World Consumption Habits Using Concat

    To create my own data observation on consumption habits - particularly mass consumption habits, I used the concat function. This enables comparision by subtopic by country. I.e. which country spent the most on clothes, against which country purchased the most clothing.

    I combined 3 sets of data to explore worldwide consumption habits during Covid (2020). By using the concat function, I was able to layer the data for best analysis. The 2020 data illustrates the significant variation in apparel purchasing habits across countries. Norway spent the most on clothes, with an average expenditure of $1,150 per person, followed closely by the UK, $950, and the USA, $900. However, Norway's spending aligns with more selective consumption, purchasing on average fewer items (15 per person). This data suggests that countries like Norway prioritises quality and invest more per garment, resulting in a higher average spend of $27 per item. Unsurprisingly, aligning with their mass consumption behaviour, the USA bought the most clothing, with 50 items per person, but spent $18 per an item (less). This reflects the trend of mass consumption, comparatively low level spending on quality and instead focusing on quantity. The UK also followed a similar pattern, with 35 items purchased per person at an average of $25 per item.
